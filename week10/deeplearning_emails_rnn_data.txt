To be sure, it's a whole bunch of related algorithms to train "deep" networks (with many hidden layers), so it highly depends on the exact structure of the network. People seem to layer different types of neural nets on top of each other, allowing further abstractions. So there's not a single algorithm that works for all network setups, in simple cases backpropagation might even work:

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep_learning#Deep_learning_in_artificial_neural_networks

Some categorisation: http://research.microsoft.com/pubs/209355/DeepLearning-NowPublishing-Vol7-SIG-039.pdf

Deep Mind, the company bought by Google for about half a billion (yes), used reinforcement learning to train the networks:

https://d1x0mwiac2rqwt.cloudfront.net/fe14d0cd886460259b7996e9d37000ce/as/1312.5602v1.pdf

So I guess it depends on how complicated you want to make it ;-)
It seems to work really great for vision problems (since human vision works similarly with many layers of abstraction in feature detection towards object recognition), and language understanding, but I don't know about current results for analysing less-human data.
